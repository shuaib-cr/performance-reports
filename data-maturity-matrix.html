§ 01

The five stages of data maturity

Company size is a weak proxy for data maturity — a 5,000-person bank can sit at Stage 1 while a 40-person AI lab operates at Stage 4. The lens that actually matters: how well does this org understand the past, monitor the present, and forecast the future using data? The five stages below progress along that axis.

STAGE 00
Reactive
Pre-Series A · <25 people · Ad-hoc SQL, founder dashboards, no dedicated data hire
STAGE 01
Repeatable
Series A–B · 25–150 people · First data hire(s), warehouse stood up, dashboards consolidated
STAGE 02
Defined
Series B–C · 150–500 people · Specialized roles, metric layer, first ML in production
STAGE 03
Managed
Series C–pre-IPO · 500–2,500 people · Federated/embedded teams, formal governance, ML platform
STAGE 04
Optimized
Public / hyperscale · 2,500+ people · Data mesh / products, CDO, treasury-grade controls
Read this first

The most expensive mistakes happen when teams adopt Stage 03 tooling at Stage 01 maturity, or staff Stage 04 roles before the organization can absorb them. Use the matrix to find your true row, then look one column to the right — that is your next 12 months.

§ 04

Three reference archetypes

Different operating models call for different center-of-gravity choices. These three archetypes — distilled from how Anthropic/OpenAI, Databricks/Snowflake-style platforms, and Netflix/Uber operate — show that "data maturity" doesn't converge on a single shape.

Archetype A · Frontier AI lab

The research-led shop

Exemplars
OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral, xAI
Center of gravity
Pre-training data + RLHF + eval; data is the moat
Org shape
Research / Pretraining / Post-training / Evals / Infra — analytics & BI are downstream
Warehouse
Often custom — petabyte object stores feeding training; Snowflake/BigQuery for finance & product analytics only
ML platform
Mostly in-house; Ray + custom orchestration on GPU/TPU clusters
Critical capability
Eval infrastructure & data quality at training scale
Mature in ML, deliberately spartan in BI. Will look "immature" on analytics tooling because they invested elsewhere.
Archetype B · Data platform vendor

The eat-your-own-dogfood

Exemplars
Databricks, Snowflake, Confluent, dbt Labs
Center of gravity
Own product as the data backbone; reference implementation pressure
Org shape
Platform team + embedded analytics; mature governance early (customers demand it)
Warehouse
Their own — Lakehouse / Snowflake. Iceberg/Delta from day one
ML platform
Mosaic AI / MLflow ecosystem; feature store, model serving, agent eval all native
Critical capability
Unified governance (Unity Catalog-style) across data + ML + AI
Most "complete" on paper. Risk is over-fitting to internal tools; what works at Databricks may be heavy for a 200-person SaaS.
Archetype C · Marketplace / consumer tech

The experiment factory

Exemplars
Netflix, Airbnb, Uber, DoorDash, Stripe
Center of gravity
Experimentation + ML for ranking/pricing/matching, at enormous scale
Org shape
Federated/mesh from Series C onward; data products owned by domains
Warehouse
Hybrid lakehouse — Iceberg + Spark + warehouse for serving
ML platform
Custom (Michelangelo, Zipline, Metaflow); feature stores serving trillions of computations
Critical capability
Experimentation platform as a first-class product
The "data mesh" archetype. Heavy investment in internal platforms only earns out beyond ~$500M revenue.
§ 05

Six operating principles

Across all archetypes, six patterns separate teams that compound from those that thrash. Use these as the inputs to your roadmap before locking in tools.

i

Maturity is not size

A 200-person AI startup can sit at Stage 03 on ML and Stage 01 on BI simultaneously. Score each capability independently, then prioritize the laggards that block the next business move — not the ones with the loudest tooling pitch.

ii

Hire ahead of one stage, never two

A Stage 01 org hiring a Head of ML Platform will create a team with no work. A Stage 01 org hiring an Analytics Engineer who can grow into the role will compound. Hire for current + 12 months, not the org chart you imagine in 2028.

iii

Tools are 5–10% of the cost

Headcount is 5–30× the tooling bill. Optimizing the SaaS line item while underspending on senior data hires is a classic Stage 01 mistake. Buy the boring stack; spend on people who design pipelines you don't have to rebuild.

iv

Centralize early, federate later

Below ~150 people, a single centralized team enforces consistent definitions faster than embedded analysts ever will. Above ~500 people, that same structure becomes the bottleneck. The handoff window is 150–300 people for most orgs.

v

Governance is a Stage 02 problem

Buying Collibra at Stage 01 is the data-team equivalent of buying SAP at Series A. The cheap version of governance — dbt tests, a metrics layer, a README — gets you 80% of the value until you have stewards to operate the heavier tools.

vi

AI doesn't skip the stages

GenAI features shipped before the data foundations exist will degrade the moment usage scales. RAG over messy docs, evals against unverified ground truth, agents over ungoverned tools — all produce confident, wrong answers. Build the floor before the penthouse.
